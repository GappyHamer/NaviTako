If you view the whole crypto market as a single map, **Bitcoin dominance (BTC Dominance)** is the share of that map Bitcoin's territory occupies. The calculation is simple: Bitcoin's market cap divided by total crypto market cap, as a percentage. Dominance at 60% means that of every 100 dollars in the market, 60 sit in Bitcoin and 40 in the thousands of other coins (altcoins).

## The two ways dominance moves

Dominance doesn't only rise when Bitcoin's price rises. Because it's a ratio, what decides it is **the relative speed of the numerator (Bitcoin) and the denominator (the whole).**

- Bitcoin rises, alts flat → dominance up
- Bitcoin falls but alts fall harder → dominance up even as prices drop
- Bitcoin rises gently but alts moon → dominance down

So the dominance chart shows information separate from the price chart — namely, **the flow of money within the market and its risk appetite.** A phase where money crowds into "relatively proven" Bitcoin often reflects the market's defensive psychology, while a phase where money spreads out to small-cap, risky alts often reflects aggressive greed.

## The classic narrative of the cycle

Past bull markets showed a repeated sequence. ① Early in a new cycle, Bitcoin runs first and dominance rises → ② while Bitcoin rests near its highs, profits rotate into large alts like Ethereum → ③ finally even small alts moon in the so-called **alt season** and dominance plunges → ④ once the party ends, alts collapse the deepest and dominance recovers again.

In the late-2017 alt frenzy, dominance dropped into the 30s, then recovered to the 60–70s through the 2018 bear market. A similar rhythm appeared in 2021. That's how the proverb "a dominance plunge is a signal of the cycle's late stage" was born. But keep in mind this is a pattern observed across just a couple of cycles — the sample is far too small to call it a statistical law.

## How stablecoins changed the reading

Today's dominance reading comes with one added caveat: the denominator, "total market cap," includes **stablecoins** like USDT and USDC. Stablecoins are pegged to one dollar — effectively cash on the sidelines. When the market flees risk by selling coins and hiding in stablecoins, that alone changes the composition of the denominator and suppresses Bitcoin dominance. So lately many people separately watch dominance excluding stablecoins, or conversely **stablecoin dominance** (which rises the greater the fear). The same number can mean slightly different things depending on the era.

## The trap of long-term comparison — 90% in 2017 vs. 60% now

One more thing. When looking at dominance's long-term chart, remember that **the composition of the denominator has kept changing.** Crypto in 2013 was essentially Bitcoin plus a handful of alts, but today tens of thousands of tokens enter the tally. Every time a new token lists, the denominator grows, so dominance carries a structural bias that drifts slowly lower over time. That's why a simple comparison like "it was 90% in 2017 but 60% now, so Bitcoin's era is fading" is dangerous. For the same reason, laying supporting indicators like Ethereum dominance and total altcoin market cap (TOTAL3) alongside and reading the relative flow gives you less distortion.

## How to make use of it

You can check dominance easily on TradingView under the `BTC.D` symbol, and in the chart menus of CoinMarketCap and CoinGecko. To stress it again, dominance is not a trade signal generator but closer to **narration describing the market's phase.** It's for reading "is the market's energy concentrating in Bitcoin right now, or has risk appetite spread all the way to the edge of alts?" If the [Fear & Greed Index](/guide/fear-greed-index) shows the temperature of emotion and the [long/short ratio](/guide/long-short-ratio) shows the lean of positions, dominance shows the topography of money. For the record, alternative.me's Fear & Greed Index already includes changes in dominance as one of its components — so the octopus on this site reads the shadow of dominance indirectly, through that path.
