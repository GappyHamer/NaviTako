Lurk in any Bitcoin community for a few days and you'll run into lines like "Fear & Greed is at 12, isn't this the bottom?" or "Greed just hit 90, starting to get nervous." The Crypto Fear & Greed Index they're talking about is **a single number between 0 and 100 that sums up the collective psychology of market participants**. The closer it is to 0, the more the market is gripped by extreme fear; the closer to 100, the more everyone is drunk on greed.

The most widely used version is the index alternative.me has published daily since 2018, and the octopus on this site reads the value from that same source. Stock markets have a similar index built by CNN, but the crypto version is tuned to the quirks of the crypto market.

## What the index is made of

The alternative.me Crypto Fear & Greed Index is a weighted average of several data streams. The publicly disclosed components look roughly like this.

- **Volatility (about 25%)**: Compares Bitcoin's recent volatility and maximum drawdown against 30-day and 90-day averages. Abnormally large volatility is read as a fear signal.
- **Market momentum / volume (about 25%)**: Compares current volume and momentum against average levels. When buy volume piles up excessively in a rally, the greed score climbs.
- **Social media (about 15%)**: Measures the number of posts on Bitcoin-related hashtags and how fast people interact with them. When suddenly everyone starts talking about Bitcoin, that's a greed signal.
- **Bitcoin dominance (about 10%)**: Bitcoin's share of total crypto market cap. Money crowding into "relatively safe" Bitcoin reads as fear; money scattering into altcoins reads as greed.
- **Google Trends (about 10%)**: Looks at the trend of negative search terms like "bitcoin price manipulation" alongside general search volume.

The operator can adjust the exact internal weights, but the core idea is one thing: **it measures people's reactions, not the price itself.**

## Why people read it as a "contrarian signal"

The reason this index got famous is the exact logic of Warren Buffett's old line: "Be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful." A market sunk in extreme fear may mean most of the sellers have already sold, and extreme greed may mean everyone who was going to buy has already bought.

Past examples are interesting. Periods when the index dropped into the single digits — late 2018, the March 2020 COVID crash, right after the FTX blowup in late 2022 — often overlapped, in hindsight, with long-term bottom zones. Conversely, near the top of the early 2021 bull run, the index sat above 90 for a good while.

## So how should you use it?

The traps are just as clear, though. First, **extremes can last far longer than you'd think.** The index crossing 80 doesn't kick off a drop immediately; it can hang in the greed zone for weeks while price keeps grinding higher. Second, this index **is not a timing tool.** It hints at direction — "a pullback will come eventually" — but it won't tell you whether that's today or three months from now. Third, the index itself includes components that lag price (volatility, volume), so it leans toward summarizing what already happened.

That's why experienced participants rarely use Fear & Greed on its own. They lay it next to other sentiment gauges like funding rate and long/short ratio, and use it as a thermometer for "how far the market is leaning one way."
