"It drops when I buy and rises when I sell" — that self-deprecating line is universal across borders. Of course, the market doesn't target any particular individual. And yet the tendency of the retail investor crowd to buy and sell at unfavorable timing **on average, and repeatedly** really is observed in study after study. Behavioral economics looks for the reason not in conspiracy but in **the default settings of the human brain.**

## 1. Loss aversion — the pain of losing is twice the joy of gaining

The core of Kahneman and Tversky's Prospect Theory is that, for the same amount of money, **the pain of a loss feels roughly twice as large as the joy of an equal gain.** This asymmetry breeds two destructive behaviors: locking in a loss is so painful that you cling endlessly to a trapped position ("diamond hands out of despair"), while gains you rush to lock in for fear they'll vanish.

## 2. The disposition effect — quick to take profit, never to cut loss

This pattern even has a name: the **disposition effect.** Odean's 1998 study found that retail investors sell winning holdings at a far higher rate than losing ones — and confirmed that the winners they dumped tended to perform better afterward. It's the exact execution of what textbooks tell you not to do: "cut your winners short, let your losses run." In the leveraged futures market, this habit often leads to the forced ending of [liquidation](/guide/futures-liquidation).

## 3. FOMO and recency bias — why you end up buying after the rise

Humans give excessive weight to recent experience (recency bias). When price rises for weeks, the brain takes "a world that keeps rising" as its default, and the fear of missing out (FOMO) presses the buy button. The trouble is that the moment when news and profit screenshots are loudest is, statistically, the late stage of the cycle. The pattern of new retail account openings and deposits crowding in when the [Fear & Greed Index](/guide/fear-greed-index) points to extreme greed is a collective expression of this bias.

## 4. Overconfidence — the illusion built by beginner's luck

In a bull market, anything you buy goes up. The brain books the gains earned here not as "thanks to the market" but as "my skill" (self-attribution bias). Overconfidence increases trading frequency and pushes up leverage — and as Barber and Odean's research shows, **the more you trade, the worse your performance gets from fees and timing costs.** As we saw in [The Math of Leverage](/guide/leverage-math), a larger multiple shrinks the survivable margin of error first, regardless of skill.

## 5. Herding — the moment everyone stands on the same side

When uncertain, humans evolved to follow the majority. Usually a reasonable energy-saving device, but in markets, the moment everyone leans the same direction creates a problem: **the counterparty to that trade disappears.** If everyone is already long, the capacity for further buying is spent, and crowded positions become fuel for a move the opposite way. The convention in [long/short ratio](/guide/long-short-ratio) of reading the global account lean contrarian is grounded in this structure.

## So how does retail get trapped less?

Bias doesn't disappear through knowledge. Kahneman himself confessed that "I wrote the book and I still fall for it." Realistic defense is not willpower but **structure.** Decide and write down your invalidation condition (stop-loss criteria) before you enter; use only an amount whose loss won't shake your life; put rules and time delays in place so decisions aren't made in a state of excitement — all devices that strip authority in advance from "the excited future you."

The fact that this site delivers its prophecy in a single word, "long" or "short," and never states a number or target price, is in the same spirit. Keep the fun in the place of fun, and let the decisions about your account be made by a cool head that understands its biases.
